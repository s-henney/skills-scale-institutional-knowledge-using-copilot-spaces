Based on the docs/ folder, OctoAcme runs projects through a clear, stage-based lifecycle that moves from initiation to close with emphasis on small, iterative deliveries. Projects start with a one-pager to capture problem, goals, success metrics, stakeholders, and a high-level timeline; a decision gate only advances work to planning once success metrics and stakeholder alignment are confirmed. Planning breaks approved initiatives into prioritized, estimable backlog items with acceptance criteria and a Definition of Done, then maps those items into timeboxed sprints or release milestones tracked on a project board (Backlog 120 Ready 120 In Progress 120 In Review 120 QA 120 Done). Pull requests are expected to be small, link to an issue and acceptance criteria, run CI/linting before review, and require at least one approval.

Roles and responsibilities are explicit so ownership and expectations are clear: Product Managers set outcomes, prioritize the backlog, and define success metrics; Project Managers coordinate delivery, schedules, and risk registers; Developers implement features, tests, and participate in reviews; QA/Testing validate acceptance criteria and manage manual verification where required; stakeholders provide input and approvals. The persona definitions are intended to be used both for real assignments and as prompts in exercises to model interactions and decision-making.

Communication and cadence are formalized to maintain alignment and surface issues quickly. The team rhythm includes daily standups for progress and blockers, weekly delivery syncs to review progress and flagged risks, demos at the end of each sprint or milestone, and monthly or milestone stakeholder updates. Templates and registers (e.g., Weekly Status, Risk Register, Release Notes) are used as single sources of truth for updates and escalation paths (team 120 PM 120 Product Lead 120 Sponsor) are defined for faster triage of blockers and incidents.

Quality assurance and release controls are emphasized across the lifecycle. Developers are expected to provide unit and integration tests, and critical flows get end-to-end smoke tests. CI enforces tests, linters, and security scanning before merges; release readiness requires passing CI, drafted release notes, and a rollback/mitigation plan. Deployment checklists call for staging verification, automated pipelines when possible, post-deploy checks, and an incident playbook that includes rollback and blameless retrospectives to capture action items and continuous improvements.
